NYC VULNERABLE ROAD USER CRASH ANALYSIS

NYC has multiple independent signals about where cyclist and pedestrian risk concentrates — infrastructure gaps, resident complaints, demographic exposure — collected by different city agencies and never cross-referenced. This project walks through all three, one at a time, then stress-tests the resulting recommendations before trusting them.

Role
Analysis & visualization
Tools
Python, GeoPandas, Leaflet
Method
Spatial joins, hypothesis testing
Course
GTECH 731, Hunter College

THE PROBLEM

Bike-lane gap streets, blocked-lane 311 complaints, and demographic risk are three separate signals collected by three different parts of city government — none of them cross-referenced against the others. Each one alone is suggestive; laid out side by side, they either corroborate or contradict each other, which is exactly what a decision-maker needs before acting on any one of them. What follows is each analysis in turn: the method, the map, and what it actually shows.

Analysis 1 of 3

BIKE LANE GAPS

Every cyclist-involved crash from 2016–2025 is spatially joined to the nearest bike lane in projected meters (EPSG:32618); accidents more than 100m away are grouped by street to surface the top 10 off-network candidates for new infrastructure. A clear "one block over" effect shows up in the results: accidents cluster 50–120m from existing lanes rather than right on top of them, often on a one-way street missing a lane on its opposite-direction neighbor. The 10 streets with the most off-network accidents — led by Broadway, Fulton Street, and 3rd Avenue — are the strongest candidates for closing that gap.

VALIDATING THE RECOMMENDATION

Before treating the top-10 off-network street list above as more than a raw tally, it was checked three independent ways.

Threshold Sensitivity
9 of 10 streets

Robust across 50m and 150m cutoff variants of the original 100m threshold. Only Linden Boulevard is threshold-sensitive — it drops out at 50m, replaced by Myrtle Avenue.

Vision Zero Cross-Check
72.5%

of the top-10 streets' off-network accidents sit on a DOT-designated Vision Zero Priority Corridor — independent external corroboration from NYC DOT's own dataset, ranging from 100% on Nostrand/Bushwick Avenues down to 53% on Broadway.

Confounding Check
ρ = 0.32

Spearman correlation (p < 0.0001, 406 of 2,388 streets matched to a monitored traffic location) between off-network accident counts and mean traffic volume — a real but moderate confound, disclosed rather than hidden, alongside its partial (17%) match-rate coverage.

Analysis 2 of 3

311 OVERLAP ZONES

"Blocked Bike Lane" 311 complaints and bike accidents are each snapped to a ~110m grid cell; cells containing both are flagged as overlap zones. Streets with more complaints also see more accidents (r = 0.43) — a moderate but real correlation pointing at illegal parking as a direct, fixable hazard rather than a coincidence.

Analysis 3 of 3

HIGH-RISK ZIP CODES

Accident counts per ZIP code are compared against population, flagging codes in the bottom 40th population percentile but top 65th accident percentile. Of the 10 ZIP codes originally flagged this way, a sensitivity check at two nearby percentile cutoffs found only 5 hold up consistently — 10001, 10012, 10013, 10017, and 11232 — so outreach and infrastructure review should prioritize those 5 first, treating the other 5 as a lower-confidence watch list rather than an equally-actionable list.

METHODOLOGY / GROUNDING

This page summarizes findings from a 45-cell analysis notebook covering 10 years of NYC crash data (2016–2025), weather correlation, bike-lane proximity, traffic-volume-normalized risk scoring with spatial-autocorrelation testing, 311 complaint cross-referencing, and ZIP-code demographic risk — including an honestly-disclosed negative result (a Local Moran's I hotspot finding that did not survive multiple-comparisons correction). There's no standalone paper for this one, unlike the Adirondack project — the full notebook is the primary source.
